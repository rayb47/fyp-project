1. Rock in Rio

{% blocktranslate %}'Rock in Rio is one of the world's largest and most famous music festivals originally founded in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil in 1985. As time passed, it has also become a prominent event in Portugal where it has been held every 2 years, since 2004. The festival is renowned for being held on a large scale, drawing top international artists across various music genres, from rock and pop, to electronic and indie.{% endblocktranslate %}

Location: It is held in Lisbon, usually at the Parque de Bela Vista, which is a large park that can accomodate the expansive stages, audience areas and entertainment zones that are required to host the festival.

Dates: Not fixed, but usually in the month of September.

2. Feast Day of St. Anthony

Also known as 'Dia de Santo António', the Feast Day of St. Anthony is one of Portugal's most cherished festivals where St.Anthony is considered the Patron Saint of lovers and the protector of lost items. The festival combines religious observance with popular festivities that reflect the deep cultural heritage and community spirit among the people.

One of the unique aspects of the Feast Day of Saint Anthony is the mass wedding ceremony, also known as 'Casamentos de Santa António' (Saint Anthony's Weddings). Every year, several selected couples are married simultaneously in a high-profile event that symbolizes love and community. Saint Anthony is revered as the match-maker saint, which makes these weddings particularly meaningful.

Location: Celebrated all over Portugal, but most ferevently in Lisbon, as that is Saint Anthony's place of birth.

Dates: June 13

3. São João

The São João festival is one of Portugal's most lively and colorful celebrations. It honors Saint John the Baptist and marks the beginning of the Summer season. It is characterized by a mix of religious and traditional festivities, which make it a significant cultural event with deep roots in Portuguese tradition.

Keeping with ancient tradition, bonfires are a central part of São João, around which people gather to celebrate, sing and jump over the flames as a symbol of protection and purification. One of the more amusing and unique traditions is the use of soft plastic hammers. Festival-goers playfully hit each other on the head with these hammers to create a friendly and joyful reaction amongst themselves.

Location: Mainly celebrated in Porto, and also other parts of Northern Portugal like Braga and Vila do Conde, although on a smaller scale.

Dates: June 23

1. Sanctuary of Christ the King

The Sanctuary of Christ the King (Portuguese: Santuário de Cristo Rei) is a Catholic monument and shrine dedicated to the Sacred Heart of Jesus Christ overlooking the city of Lisbon situated in Almada, in Portugal. It was inspired by the Christ the Redeemer statue of Rio de Janeiro, in Brazil, after the Cardinal Patriarch of Lisbon visited that monument. The project was inaugurated on 17 May 1959. The giant statue was erected to express gratitude because the Portuguese were spared the direct destructive effects of World War II.

2. Belém Tower

Belém Tower (Portuguese: Torre de Belém, pronounced [ˈtoʁɨ ðɨ βɨˈlɐ̃j]; literally: Bethlehem Tower), officially the Tower of Saint Vincent (Portuguese: Torre de São Vicente) is a 16th-century fortification located in Lisbon that served as a point of embarkation and disembarkation for Portuguese explorers and as a ceremonial gateway to Lisbon. It was built during the height of the Portuguese Renaissance, and is a prominent example of the Portuguese Manueline style, but it also incorporates hints of other architectural styles. The structure was built from lioz limestone and is composed of a bastion and a 30-metre (100 ft), four-storey tower.
